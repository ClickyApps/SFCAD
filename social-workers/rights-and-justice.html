Social workers

Rights, Justice and Economic Wellbeing

Advance human rights and promote social justice and economic well-being.

Social workers recognise the fundamental principles of human rights and equality, and that these are protected in national and international law, conventions and policies. They ensure these principles underpin their practice. Social workers understand the importance of using and contributing to case law and applying these rights in their own practice. They understand the effects of oppression, discrimination and poverty.

  • Routinely integrate the principles of and entitlements to social justice, social inclusion and equality, and with support, consider how and when challenge may be needed
  • Routinely apply the law to protect and advance people’s rights and entitlements, identifying and highlighting situations where interpretations of the law are neither proportionate nor fair to promote autonomy and self-determination
  • Apply the principles and entitlements of human and civil rights to analyse, evaluate and challenge interventions that are unlawful and / or disproportionate
  • Analyse differing needs, perspectives and competing rights and apply to practice
  • Enable and support people to consider and pursue a range of options that may enhance economic status (through access to education, work, housing, health services and welfare benefits)
  • Where appropriate, set up and/ or enable access to effective independent advocacy

An understanding of human rights and equality law is critical to demonstrate this capability and you will need to show how you apply your understanding in practice through the use of a case example or two. It could be argued that this capability is broader in that it includes social justice as well as rights and entitlements. What is socially justice is a moral question and it maybe that current rights and entitlements are insufficient to meet broader requirements for justice. The other element in this capability is economic wellbeing and you may want to consider cases where you have help service users to improve their economic wellbeing or perhaps where you have provided users with independent advocacy.

The following example is provided as an illustration:

In my work with adults with learning disabilities I sometimes have to undertake Best Interests Assessments and in doing so I have to exercise real care to ensure that I do not infringe a person’s human rights. In assessing Tom, a 36 year man with several autism, who had become physically unwell, I was being asked to determine whether he should be taken to hospital for treatment. He was showing signs of reluctance to go but his health was deteriorating and I recognised that he needed medical help. I explored first how much medical assistance could be given at his residential home and I put some pressure on his GP to do just that. I knew from previous work with Tom that he would become very distressed if his usual routine was disrupted. I considered all of the criteria mentioned in s4 of the Mental Capacity Act 2005. In addition, I specifically consulted Tom’s Independent Mental Capacity Advocate to ensure that Tom was able to fully participate in the decision so far as he was practically able to do and so that I considered Tom’s wishes and feelings. I knew that he would be reluctant to have his routine disrupted but I also could see that he was suffering with poor health. Eventually, I did decide that it was in his Best Interests to be taken to hospital for a short time so that medical treatment could be given. I recorded this as accurately as I could. I then prepared the hospital for Tom’s admission so that they knew how to cope with his needs and so that he could be discharged as quickly as possible.
